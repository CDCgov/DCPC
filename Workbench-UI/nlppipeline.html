2. The NLP Pipeline

NLP relies on a linear processing stream of modules each serving a particular function. This sequence forms a pipeline where the output of one module becomes the input to another. In this section, learn about some of the key NLP modules and why a linear pipeline approach is used in NLP.

What is a Software Pipeline?

In software engineering, a pipeline consists of a chain of processing elements where the output of each element is the input of the next - this process is similar to an assembly line.

NLP is a complex task, however, NLP solutions can be broken into a number of steps where each step can be addressed independently and solved separately by different specialists. In addition, this separation of function is important for maintaining software that has different functions but also because experimental work is needed on each of these functions independently of each other. This makes a pipeline a natural solution where the output of one step in the chain is the input of the next step in the chain.

A Typical NLP Pipeline

Overview

Tokeniser

A typical NLP pipeline needs to begin with identifying the basic building blocks of language, usually understood as words. In written text there are items besides words that carry information such as numbers, punctuation, abbreviations and acronyms. The total collection of words and other items in written text are called tokens.

A token is typically defined as any collection of characters between whitespace. This is not strictly true as separate tokens can be connected across certain characters such as the digits either side of a decimal point or words joined by a hyphen. The process of identifying the basic elements of a text is called tokenisation and developers can differ in their determination of tokens based on the domain and text being analyzed.

Sentence Boundary Detector (SBD)

(also known as Sentence Splitter)

Once the Tokenizer has done its work it is essential to separate the text into sentences. This step of the pipeline is known as a sentence boundary detector (SBD) or a sentence splitter. It is common to understand the end of a sentence is defined by a full stop followed by a whitespace followed by a Capital letter. However, this is not always the case especially in clinical text where fullstops are often omitted, new sentences can be defined by a newline indented in from the left margin with or without a bullet point marker. As with the Tokenizer the SBD may need to be tailored to match the nature of the text being analyzed.

Part of Speech Tagger (POS)

Once the sentences are defined it is often helpful to identify the Part of Speech (POS) each token is serving in a sentence. This process can be achieved by looking up words in a dictionary or applying a grammatical parser. Either way the processor has to deal with unknown tokens, that is tokens which are not present in the processing system lexicon. It is typical of POS taggers to assign Noun to any unknown words but that may not be adequate for some applications.

Chunker

After the POS Tagger, pipelines can diverge in many directions depending on the need. Typically if the task is to identify entities or concepts in the text then a simple parsing process called chunking can be applied. This is a process that divides a sentence of words into groupings typically representative of clauses and phrases. This is not a full grammatical parser but a simpler approximation.

Application Specific Processing

After the Chunking step, the pipeline will diverge into application specific processing. In clinical entity recognition, the chunks could be passed to a semantic recognition module, which can identify clinical phrases such as names of diseases, procedures and body sites, or into a machine learning induced language model designed to recognize specific types of entities.

Important Note: Rules for recognizing particular chunks of text can be inserted at different points throughout the pipeline depending on other dependencies in the solution design.

Pipeline Performance & Accuracy

Building a pipeline for practical use in the workplace requires understanding how accurately it performs. If it is not sufficiently accurate it may be a hindrance rather than a help in the workplace. Many simple NLP tasks can be built quickly but usually such solutions have limited accuracy and only perform at 70-80% accuracy. The effort of increasing the accuracy above 80% is normally substantial where the effort increases logarithmically the higher the performance. This means the effort to get from 80% to 90% accuracy will be substantial then twice that again to get to 95% and twice that again to get to 97.5%. It is unheard of for applications to reach above the 97.5% level if they are performing a complicated task.

Accuracy is Measured in Three Major Ways

The design and implementation of performance evaluation mechanisms is a key component in the NLP Web Service platform and workbench. Three common methods to measure accuracy are listed below.

1. Ten-fold cross validation (10CV)
Ten-fold cross validation involves dividing the training corpus into 10 sections, training the system on nine-tenths and then testing it on the tenth. This is repeated 10 time each time holding out a different one-tenth. The final accuracy is the average of the ten determinations.

2. Held out data set (HODS)
Held out data set requires developing a separate corpus of records by the client which the developer never sees. When the pipeline development has reached a suitable stage the client tests it against their held out data set and reports back to the development team the accuracy of the results.

3. Self test (ST)
Self test is the simplest method where the texts used to build the pipeline are assessed for how accurately they match they computed outputs. Although this method is not considered acceptable by researchers its value is that it gives a first approximation on the accuracy of the system and so has great practical value in identifying weaknesses in the processing stream.

Each method has its strengths and weaknesses although the Held out data set gives the client more confidence in the quality of the product they are receiving as they are the ones conducting the independent tests.

Gold Standard

All three accuracy methods, Ten-fold cross validation, Held out data set and Self test, require the preparation of a Gold Standard (GS) where the correct results are known.

Preparing a Gold Standard is a time consuming task. Most applications are only tested on a small subset of the content they potentially have to process. This incomplete testing can quickly lead to disappointment in a NLP application if client expectations are not well managed. As needed an iterative revision process must be carefully planned and executed.

Machine Rules Fusion

Machine learning methods and Rule-based methods have strengths and weaknesses. For the safety surveillance data processing, a method was adopted to combine the outputs from ETHER (a rule-based system) and the machine learning model (CRF/LibSVM).

As an example of the strength of this approach, the machine learning models missed many VACCINE or DRUG annotations, while ETHER, the rule-based system, performed much better since its lexicon covers most of the vaccines and drugs. A method was adopted to use VACCINE/DRUG outputs with ETHER outputs in place of machine learning outputs, and the performance was significantly improved.
